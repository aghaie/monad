## 2. Necessity test: Zipf vs uniform

Synthetic activation matrices were generated by sampling roots proportional to (a)
their real Zipfian frequencies and (b) uniform frequencies, then measuring the
maximum concept activation share:

| Root-frequency regime | Max concept activation share |
|---|---:|
| **Zipfian (real)** | **0.87** |
| **Uniform** | **0.21** |

- Under the **real Zipfian** frequencies, a single concept reaches ~87% activation —
  a hub emerges.
- Under **uniform** root frequencies, the maximum concept share collapses to ~21% —
  **no hub emerges**; concept shares are governed only by member-root count.

**The heavy lexical tail is necessary for hub emergence.** Remove it (flatten the
frequencies) and the hub disappears.

## 3. Necessary and sufficient conditions

| Condition | Type |
|---|---|
| A heavy-tailed (Zipfian) root-frequency distribution | **necessary** |
| A concept that aggregates the head of that distribution | **sufficient** |

Given both, a hub is **inevitable**: the head of the Zipf distribution dominates
activation, and whichever concept contains it becomes the hub. Neither condition
involves the relational structure (proposition graph, motifs, SCCs) — hub emergence
is a **lexical** phenomenon.
